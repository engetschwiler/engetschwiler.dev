**TV Time shut down on July 15, 2026. Twenty-six million people lost their watchlists overnight. Popcorn is the app they should have been using all along: free forever, no ads, no tracking, and now with full series tracking.**

## What happened

On July 2, 2026, Whip Media announced that TV Time, one of the largest TV-tracking communities ever built, with more than 26 million lifetime installs, would be discontinued. The service ended after July 15. The app was pulled from the App Store and Google Play. The website went offline. And every personal account's data was permanently deleted, with no recovery path.

The reason given was blunt: running TV Time as a free app was no longer sustainable, and there wasn't enough demand for a paid one. The parent company had been acquired by a lender in early 2025 and was redirecting itself toward enterprise AI products.

Read that again. For fifteen years, millions of people handed over their viewing habits, their episode check-ins, their ratings and reactions, and when the app stopped being useful to the business, the business deleted them.

Community comments, episode reactions, polls, the GIFs people spent years posting: none of it was even exportable. It's just gone.

That's not a technical failure. That's what happens when the people using an app were never the customer.

## What TV Time users actually want now

If you've spent the last two weeks reading migration threads, the same three things come up over and over:

1. **Get my history somewhere safe.** Years of episodes. Non-negotiable.
2. **Give me a tracker I actually enjoy opening.** Not a database with a login screen.
3. **Never let this happen again.** No lock-in, no rug-pull, no "we're pivoting to AI."

The big incumbent, Trakt, does the first one well: its importer is the most reliable in the category. But it's honest to say that most people treat Trakt as plumbing rather than a home. Its own users describe the mobile experience as functional rather than delightful, and the last few years brought a VIP price increase, new limits on the free tier, and a redesign that a vocal chunk of its longtime community openly disliked. Simkl's servers have buckled under the migration surge. Serializd is lovely, but it's web-first.

So the utility is covered. The *home* is not.

That's the gap Popcorn was built for.

Not free-with-an-asterisk. Not freemium-until-we-need-a-new-revenue-line. Free, because the app is a craft project from a studio that makes its living elsewhere, not a data pipeline dressed up as a consumer app.

Here's the uncomfortable question you should be asking every app courting TV Time refugees this month: **what's your business model, and what happens to my data when it stops working?**

TV Time's answer, in the end, was "we delete it."

Popcorn's answer is simpler because the incentives are different. There's no ad network to feed. There's no analytics SDK harvesting your viewing habits for a media-intelligence product. There's no VC clock ticking toward a monetization pivot. The app is small, it's independent, it's Swiss-made, and it's built by people who use it themselves.

That's not a growth strategy. It's just the reason it'll still be here.
